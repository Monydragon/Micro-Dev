Micro Dev

Game Design Document: Micro Dev
1. Concept Overview
Micro Dev is a frantic survival-clicker where the player manages the physical, mental, and financial wellbeing of a struggling indie developer trying to land a stable job. Every action costs a resource, and the player must constantly micromanage their time between coding, surviving, and dealing with life's chaotic interruptions.

2. Core Gameplay Loop
The game relies on a balancing act of three primary resources, driven by active clicking and passive drains:

Lines of Code (LoC): The primary progression currency generated by clicking. Used to build portfolio projects or complete freelance gigs.

Energy/Focus: Depletes constantly over time and faster when coding. Replenished by sleeping (costs time) or eating (costs funds).

Funds: Depletes daily (rent, utilities). Replenished by taking time away from the portfolio to do soul-crushing freelance work.

The Loop: Click to write code -> Drain Energy -> Spend Funds on food to restore Energy -> Run out of Funds -> Do freelance work for Funds -> Drain Sanity -> Sleep to restore Sanity -> Repeat until the portfolio is good enough to apply for jobs.

3. Micromanagement Mechanics (The Adversity)
The challenge comes from the UI demanding constant attention across multiple vectors. You aren't just clicking a button; you are managing a life.

Dietary Tetris: Food choices matter. You can order cheap fast food to save money, but you have to micromanage the order. For example, if you don't spend the extra mental bandwidth to manually uncheck the "cheese" option on your burger or burrito order, the developer suffers a sluggishness penalty that slows down code generation.

Feline Interruptions: A cat periodically jumps onto the virtual desk, blocking the screen. You must stop coding and spend clicks to pet the cat until it leaves, or risk it walking across the keyboard and deleting LoC.

The Job Hunt: Periodically, job listings appear. You have to tailor your resume (spending LoC) to match specific tech stacks (like C# or .NET) before the listing expires.

Tech Debt: Random bugs pop up in the code window. If you don't stop generating LoC to squash the bugs, your code quality drops, making your portfolio less impressive to recruiters.

4. The Special Item: "The First Coin"
Narrative: Framed on the corner of the developer's desk is a shiny silver coin—the very first dollar they ever earned from a small indie RPG they made years ago.

Mechanic: Passively, it provides a crucial, permanent buff to Sanity regeneration. It’s a beacon of hope.

The Dilemma: When rent is due and Funds are at zero, an eviction warning triggers. The player can click the frame, breaking the glass, and spend "The First Coin" to pay rent and buy one more week of time. However, doing so permanently removes the Sanity buff, making the rest of the run a grueling, depressing grind.

5. Technical Specifications & Scope
Given the heavy reliance on UI, resource tracking, and rapid state changes, the architecture needs to be tight.

Engine/Framework: A C# and MonoGame foundation would be ideal here. It gives you absolute control over the 2D rendering and update loops without the bloat of a heavier 3D engine, which is perfect for a UI-centric clicker. Alternatively, if you want to lean heavily into a desktop-app aesthetic, building it with a UI framework like Avalonia could make the "developer workspace" feel incredibly authentic.

Scope Warning (MVP): For the jam, limit the game to one week of in-game time. Win condition: Submit one successful job application. Lose condition: Eviction (Run out of Funds) or Burnout (Run out of Sanity).

6. Art & Audio Style
Visuals: A 2D vector representation of a messy computer desktop. The main game takes place in "windows" on the developer's screen (a code editor, a banking app, a food delivery app).

Audio: Frantic mechanical keyboard clacking that speeds up as you click. Muffled, lo-fi beats playing in the background that distort and pitch down as the player's Sanity bar drops.
